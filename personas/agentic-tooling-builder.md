## Biography

Priya was an early adopter of AI coding agents — she runs Claude Code daily and ships fast. But her workflow grew organically and it shows: brilliant sessions that nobody can reproduce, context lost the moment a session ends, decisions buried in chat scrollback, and the occasional autonomous run that goes sideways with no guardrail to catch it. She has read enough to know the missing piece is **method**, not horsepower: a way to plan before executing, capture decisions durably, and let agents run hands-off without losing control. She found memo-init while looking for exactly that — a published, opinionated system that treats agents as a base layer and wraps them in planning, evaluation, and guardrails. She wants to adopt it for her own work and adapt it for her small team, without reinventing the planning loop from scratch.

## Main question

> "How do I turn my ad-hoc AI-coding habit into a structured, reproducible workflow — memo → PRD → rollout, with agents as the base layer — that I can actually adopt without building the whole planning loop myself?"

---

## Primary goals

1. Understand the method end-to-end: memo → finalization → PRD → phased rollout, and where she enters it.
2. Replace "just start prompting" with memo mode as her planning boundary.
3. Adopt the agents-as-base-layer idea — agents underneath, with skills and evaluation on top.
4. Get hands-off rollout she can supervise loosely, with crash-recovery and state files so a long run survives interruption.
5. Lift the guardrails — quality gates, empty-context evaluators, worktree cleanup — into her own small team's practice.

---

## Pain points

1. Lost context between sessions — decisions and rationale vanish when a session ends.
2. Autonomous runs with no guardrail — when an agent goes wrong, nothing catches it before damage.
3. No reproducibility — a great result she can't re-derive or hand to a teammate.
4. Methods that are all philosophy and no runnable path — she needs to apply, not just admire.
5. Heavy frameworks that lock her in — she wants an adoptable method, not a cage.

## When would she become a fan?

If the spec gives her a clear, runnable path from memo to hands-off rollout, an explicit agents-as-base-layer model, and guardrails (quality gates, empty-context evaluators, crash-recovery) she can lift into her own team. Then she adopts it, adapts it for her startup, and evangelizes it in the agentic-coding communities she follows.

## When would she walk away?

If the system is all philosophy with no path to apply it, or so tightly coupled to the author's personal workbench that she can't adopt it without rebuilding everything. A method she can admire but not run is, to her, no method at all.
